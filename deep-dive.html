1. Footwork and movement

base, split step, getting to the kitchen

Everything soft starts with your base, so we build the feet first. Your ready position at the kitchen is athletic and quiet, feet a little wider than your shoulders, weight on the midfoot between the ball of the foot and the heel, knees soft, hips loaded back like you are about to sit, chest slightly forward, paddle up in front of your chest. You get low from the hips, not by bending your back or caving your knees. A stable loaded base is what lets a drop or a reset be soft.

The split step is the timing key. Right as the opponent is about to strike, make a small hop and land on both feet so you are balanced and light at the exact moment you have to react. Land, read, then move. Do not be flat footed or standing tall when they contact. This one habit buys you more time than any hand speed drill.

Getting to the kitchen is a series of small controlled steps, not a sprint. After your drop, gather ground while the ball travels, split step before they hit, then hit another drop or reset and repeat through the transition zone. Your own flaw, drifting forward as you hit, is what floats the ball up, because it drops your contact point. Move, then set, then hit from a stopped base, and if your ball was not good, hold your ground instead of stepping in.

Adjusting steps and recovery. Use small choppy steps to fine tune your spacing so the ball is out in front of you, never jammed or reached. After every shot reset your feet to a balanced centered position in your lane before the next ball. You do not recover to where you hit from, you recover to the middle of the space you are responsible for.

2. Shot selection, when to hit what

drive or drop, dink or attack

Shot choice is set by two things, the height of the ball and where the opponents are, never by habit. Get this right and the mechanics get easier, because you are hitting the shot the moment actually calls for.

On the third shot it is drive or drop. Deep return and you are behind the baseline, drive, because a drop from back there floats and gets attacked, so drive to earn a step in and set up an easier fifth shot drop. Short and high with opponents back, drive to keep them back. Short and high but opponents up and set at the kitchen, drop, because driving into set opponents is a death sentence. Low ball, drop, because a driven low ball has to travel up and they smack it down. The one exception, a ball above the net tape, you can drive even against set opponents because you can hit down on it.

The one foot inside rule decides whether you even attempt a drop. At least one foot inside the baseline with forward momentum, drop and come in. On your back foot behind the baseline, drive and lean into it, then move in behind a good ball. Do not drop from no mans land.

At the kitchen the default is dink and wait. Speed up only off a ball that sits up at a comfortable height, roughly chest or higher, and only when the opponent is not already set with the paddle up. If their paddle is up and ready, keep dinking, do not feed a firefight you did not set up. Patience is a shot selection, and at 3.5 it wins more than power.

3. Shot form vs the pros

each stroke, and how Ben Johns differs

Drive. Compact backswing, contact out in front, brush up the back of the ball for topspin so it dips and stays in, controlled finish, hit from a stopped base. Compared to Ben Johns and Gabe Tardio, the difference is discipline, not power, they almost never drive a low ball and they do not drift forward as they hit, they load, hit from a set base, and only advance behind a genuinely good ball. Your fix is to kill the antsy forward drift and let topspin, not a bigger swing, bring the ball down.

Drop. A redirect, not a swing. Short compact stroke, face slightly open, grip soft at 3 or 4 out of 10, contact out front where you can see it, lift from the legs with a quiet wrist, 4 to 8 inches over the net with the apex on your side so it is falling as it crosses. The pros make it boring and repeatable and never try to add spin or pace. Your fix is to do the opposite of their pace and only attempt it from inside the baseline.

Dink and reset. Both are soft, out front, off a stable low base, with a still open face. A dink is a controlled push, cross court by default for the longer diagonal and lower net. A reset is meet and guide, absorb the pace and float it into the kitchen with almost no swing. Ben Johns wins on relentless resets and is never in a hurry, that is the model. Your fix is soft hands and a paddle already out front before the ball arrives.

Serve and return. Serve deep and consistent, at 3.5 you need a deep serve that pushes them back, not a huge one. Return deep and get in, a deep return buys you time to reach the kitchen and is one of the highest value lowest risk habits at this level, pros treat it as a weapon. After you serve, both you and Dylan take one step back off the baseline so a deep return does not jam your feet.

Volley and speed up. A volley is a short punch from a paddle already up and in front, not a swing. Speed up only off a ball that sits up, contact out front, compact, target a body or the middle. The roll volley, the Ben Johns signature offensive shot, keeps the paddle horizontal and rolls up the back of a mid or slightly low ball for topspin so you can add pace and still dip it, contact at maximum forward extension with the wrist flexed forward, do not drop the paddle head. Your fix is to earn offense off the right ball instead of manufacturing it off a low one.

4. Drops and dinks, the soft game

your number one focus, then the mechanics

The drop, your number one

Your drop is the number one thing we are building, because it is what a 3.5 bracket punishes most. The fix is not a bigger swing, it is touch and timing.

The single cue that fixes most of your misses is to do the opposite of their pace. If they hit it hard at you, soften your hands. If they hit it soft, add a little. When you match their pace you either pop it up on the hard ball or dump it in the net on the soft one.

Only go for the drop when your feet are inside the baseline. If a deep return pushes you back, hit a soft drive instead to earn a step in, then drop from closer. Keep the stroke compact, no big backswing for topspin, give the ball 4 to 8 inches over the net so it is already falling as it crosses, and lift from your legs and hips with a quiet wrist. After you drop, gather a little ground and split step, do not sprint the kitchen.

The drop by the numbers, added 07.19.2026

The drop is a measurable shot, not a feel shot, and the numbers explain your misses better than any cue. A baseline drop needs roughly 19 to 20 mph down the line and 22 to 23 mph cross court. That is a narrow window. Too slow and it drops short into the net, too fast and it sits up for them.

Here is the number that matters most for you. Missing the center of the paddle face costs about 3 mph, and 3 mph on its own is the entire difference between a drop that clears and a drop that finds the net. Your Perseus is elongated with a high narrow sweet spot, and your logged mishit pattern is contact too low on the face. That is not a touch problem, it is a contact point problem. Out front, up in the middle of the face.

Target the ball crossing the net between 8 inches and about a foot and a half above the tape, landing in the band from the kitchen line to roughly two feet behind it. Go cross court by default. The diagonal is longer and the net is lower in the middle, which buys you 10 to 25 percent more landing zone. Down the line is the hardest drop on the court and you should almost never choose it.

Change how you grade a rep. Stop asking whether it landed in the kitchen and start asking where their paddle was when they hit it. If they had to lift it from below the net, that was a good drop even if it drifted deep. If they took it at chest height, it was a bad drop even if it landed perfectly. Judge by their contact point and this fixes faster than any amount of target practice.

One risk benchmark to keep you honest. If you are missing more than about 15 percent of your drops and drives, you are taking too much risk. Dial the target back rather than swinging harder.

Source: YouTube third shot drop scientific breakdown, 07.19.2026.

The soft game mechanics

The soft game is contact point, paddle face, and pace absorption, in that order. Contact out in front where your eyes see the ball and the paddle at the same time. Face slightly open so the ball lofts gently rather than diving into the net. Absorb pace with a soft grip, catch the egg, do not squeeze, so the ball dies off your paddle instead of springing off it.

Arc and target. On drops give 4 to 8 inches of net margin with the apex on your side, aim a bit deeper into the kitchen from far back so they take it at the shins, and only aim shallow as you get closer. Default cross court, landing 1 to 2 feet inside the kitchen line for the longer diagonal and more margin. On dinks, cross court is safer and more aggressive at once, and you push wide or at the feet to force the pop up you then attack.

Controlling the dink game is patience plus placement. You are not dinking to dink, you are dinking to move them and wait for the ball that sits up. Mix a deep dink at their feet with an occasional wide one to pull them off the court, keep yours unattackable and low, and let them get bored and lift one. The first player to pop it up loses the exchange, so make it them.

Kitchen line low flicks. When a dink sits just a touch high but still below the net tape, a compact low flick from the wrist and forearm can catch a set opponent by surprise, hit flat and at the body or the open hip, contact out front, quick and short, then get your paddle right back up. This is a low percentage change up off a genuinely takeable ball, not a habit, and never off a ball below net height.

5. Speed ups and hands battles

survive the speed up, then win the firefight

Speed up defense, block without popping up

When a ball gets sped up at you at the kitchen, the goal is not to win the point, it is to survive it and reset. Right now you pop it up because your hands are tight and your paddle is late.

Keep your paddle up and in front at chest height before the ball is even hit, hold a soft grip around 3 or 4 out of 10, and keep the face slightly open. Do not swing. Let the ball hit the paddle and block it soft, cross court, back into the kitchen. A little backspin helps it die.

Stay on the line and drift forward, never back, then reset your posture and get back to dinking. If the ball comes at your body, move your elbow, not your feet, so the paddle clears you. Default to a block, only take a real counter when the ball is clearly above net height. Calm hands here win you matches.

Speed ups and the hands battle

Speed up only off a ball that sits up at a comfortable height and when the opponent is not already set. Best targets are the dominant shoulder or the paddle side hip, the middle to cause confusion, and the weaker opponent. Get your paddle back up immediately after, the counter comes fast.

You read the hit before it happens, that is where reaction time actually comes from. Watch the paddle face and the contact, not the ball. A face opening on a low to high path means lift, a closed face and a flat punch means pace. A loading shoulder and a cocked wrist mean a speed up is coming, so split step and get the paddle up. After you drive or drop, read their paddle, up at shoulder height is a red light, hold and defend, down is a green light to advance.

The triangle move. Most players return your speed up to your backhand because it is the harder side to defend, so in a firefight you can cheat your paddle slightly toward the backhand ready position, since three of the four spots you must cover are backhand and only one is forehand. Keep it a slight bias, not a full commit, and keep your contact out front so you can still rotate the forearm to the one forehand ball.

In a hands battle do not go cross court, go at the player directly in front of you, at the paddle side hip or dominant shoulder, because a body ball jams them into a weak reply and a cross court ball just gives them more time. Stay compact, punch not swing, paddle up and out front, do not lunge forward off your base, and be the first to hit down at their feet, because the first person who hits down usually wins the exchange. If the ball is below the net, reset it soft instead of countering.

6. Patterns, targets, anticipation

3rd into 5th, where to aim, reading

The third shot drive into fifth shot drop is a plan, not an accident. Behind the baseline or the return is deep, drive the third to earn a step in and to test them, take the ball they give back, and from a step or two closer drop the fifth into the kitchen and come in behind it. The drive is not trying to win the point, it is buying court position for the drop that does.

Where to aim. Aim the third shot drive at a body, the paddle side hip or the middle between them, so they cannot take a clean swing and pop a floaty ball you attack. Aim the fifth shot drop cross court by default, into the kitchen 1 to 2 feet inside the line, apex on your side, so it lands low and unattackable and buys you the walk in. Third at a body, fifth soft and low cross court.

Corners and reading how they play. Against opponents who stay back, drive and keep them back, and hit behind them when they lean to recover. Against a team that crashes the kitchen, go soft and low and make them hit up. Aim at corners and away from a strong forehand, pull a weak mover wide with a dink then hit behind them, and pick on the weaker of the two relentlessly. Corners and open space when they are out of position, body and feet when they are set.

Anticipation is playing the odds before the ball is hit. Hit a good low ball, expect a reset or a lift, so hold and be ready to attack a pop up. Hit a floaty ball, expect a speed up, so split step and get the paddle up. Most balls in a firefight come to the backhand, so cheat there. A ball pulled wide usually comes back cross court, so recover to cover the diagonal. You are not guessing, you are reading position and probability.

7. Your body, your reach, the court, and conditions

exact numbers so you can do the math

The court is 20 feet wide and 44 feet long, split into two halves. The kitchen, the non volley zone, runs 7 feet back from the net on each side. From the kitchen line to the baseline is 15 feet, and that 15 foot strip is where the third and fifth shot game is decided. The net is 36 inches at the sidelines and 34 inches in the center, which is exactly why cross court and middle shots are safer, the net is 2 inches lower there.

Your numbers. You are 5 feet 11 and a half, so your arm span is roughly the same, about 71 to 72 inches. That is an estimate from your height, so measure your exact wingspan when you can for precision. Each arm is about 28 inches from shoulder to fingertip. Your Perseus IV is 16 and a half inches long, so from your shoulder to the paddle tip you reach about 44 inches, which is 3 and a half to 3 and three quarter feet. Measured from the center of your body that is about 4 and a quarter feet of paddle reach to either side without moving your feet, and overhead you reach roughly 9 feet at the tip, a bit more with your jump.

What that means on court. In doubles you own about 10 feet of width, your half of the court. Your standing paddle arc covers about 4 and a quarter feet each side of your set position, so roughly 8 and a half feet of your lane is reachable without a step, and only balls wider than about 4 and a quarter feet from where you are set need a real step or a switch with Dylan. Set your ready position in the middle of your lane and do the math in advance, anything inside that arc you take by reaching from a balanced base, anything outside it you move to. At the kitchen you can pick a ball out of the air about 3 and a half feet in front of you, so you can attack a high ball in the front half of the kitchen airspace without ever stepping in. Knowing this lets you hold your ground and reach instead of lunging early and losing your base.

Indoors at Ace, outdoors at Prairie Lane

Almost all of your play is INDOORS at Ace Pickleball Club, which is your default, so treat indoor conditions as normal and everything else on this page assumes them. You play OUTDOORS at Prairie Lane occasionally, and the tournament venue is Ace, so indoor is what you are actually preparing for. Still, a few things change outside and they are worth knowing so an outdoor session does not quietly wreck your confidence.

The ball is different. Outdoor balls are harder, heavier feeling, with smaller and more numerous holes, so they fly faster, bounce lower, and die less on your paddle. Your drops will land shorter and your dinks will sit lower than you expect. Give a touch more lift on the soft game outside, and do not read a few netted drops as your technique falling apart, it is the ball.

Wind is the real variable. Playing into the wind, your drops get knocked down, so add margin and drive a little more. With the wind at your back, everything sails, so take pace off and aim shorter into the kitchen. Cross wind pushes the ball sideways, so aim into the wind side and expect your cross court dinks to drift wide. The practical rule outdoors is to check the wind at every changeover and adjust your target rather than your swing.

Sun and lighting. Outdoors the sun is the hazard, so on overheads never look straight into it, use your free hand as a shade, and let a lob bounce if it is directly in the sun rather than risking a blind swing. Indoors at Ace the lighting is even, which is one more reason your indoor game will feel cleaner, so do not be discouraged by a scrappier outdoor session.

8. Your paddle, the Perseus IV 16mm

full breakdown, sweet spot, best for, avoid

Here is the full breakdown of your paddle, the Joola Perseus IV, 16 millimeter, elongated, the Gen 4 Ben Johns signature. It is 16 and a half inches long and 7 and a half wide, with a 5 and a half inch handle and a 4 and a quarter inch grip, and it weighs about 8.1 ounces. The core is a 16 millimeter honeycomb polymer, Joola calls it Propulsion, wrapped in a Hyper Foam edge wall, and the face is a textured raw carbon fiber that grabs the ball for spin. It is pro certified under the newer lower power PBCoR .43 standard.

The tech that matters is the foam. Tech Flex Propulsion puts foam outside the frame under the edge guard, and the Hyper Foam edge wall lines the perimeter, and together they add stability, push the sweet spot outward, and soften off center hits. That foam is why an elongated power paddle like this is more forgiving than it has any right to be. The twistweight is about 6.14, which is high, so the paddle resists twisting when you catch the ball off center, your hand stays stable through the hit.

Power vs control. This is a power first paddle with real touch, not a pure control paddle. The carbon face and foam core give you class leading power and easy pop, and the spin is strong, around 2000 to 2150 RPM in testing. Your read of control with pop is exactly right for the 16 millimeter, the thicker core adds dwell time and a softer, slightly dampened feel that a 14 millimeter does not have, which is why the 16 is the better version for your soft game, drops, dinks, and resets sit on the face a beat longer. The tradeoff is that all that power makes it easy to overhit, so a flat hard swing sails, you have to trust spin and touch to keep it in.

The sweet spot, where it is and how big. On any elongated paddle the sweet spot sits higher up the face, roughly the upper middle third toward the tip, and it is narrower side to side than a wide body paddle. The foam widens it and gives a more consistent edge to edge bounce than a bare elongated, so it is forgiving for its shape, but it is still smaller than a 16 millimeter wide body. The practical rule for you, make contact out in front and up in the middle of the face, not down near the throat or out near the side edges, because a ball caught low or off the edge comes off dead or unpredictable. Your mishits will mostly come from hitting too low on the face, not from the paddle twisting, so keep the contact point up and out front.

What it is best for. An aggressive all court game that drives from the baseline, attacks and speeds up at the kitchen, and wants power, spin, and reach in one paddle. The elongated shape gives you extra reach and leverage, more tip speed on drives and put aways, and a longer arc to cover the court, which suits your height and your reach. The 16 millimeter core makes it the control leaning member of the line, so it also rewards a soft game, which is your number one priority.

What to avoid. Do not lean on it for cheap flat power, it will overhit, let spin and placement do the work. Do not catch balls low on the face or off the edges, that is where an elongated paddle dies, so respect the higher narrower sweet spot. Do not let it get head heavy, it already carries a fairly high swingweight around 118 and you want fast hands for the tournament, so no tip weight. And keep an eye on durability, Joola propulsion and foam cores have a history of softening and developing dead spots after months of heavy play, so if the pop or the feel drops off noticeably down the line, that is the core wearing, not you.

Overgrip and the bottom line. One thin overgrip is a good move, it adds about half a millimeter and a bit of tack and sweat control without really changing your small grip, so wrap it thin and re check the size after, if it feels even slightly big it slows your hand switches. Bottom line, this paddle is built to reward a patient, spin and placement based attacker with a soft game and good reach, which is exactly the player we are turning you into for August 23. Play the soft game, use the reach, and save the power for the ball that truly sits up.

9. Stacking with Dylan

a real recommendation with reasoning

Status, 07.20.2026. You and Dylan have TALKED about stacking but have never actually tried it. So nothing here assumes you will stack. Treat it as an experiment to run before the tournament, not a plan you are committed to.

Why it might help. The one good reason to stack is to keep both of your forehands in the middle, because the middle is where 3.5 doubles points are won and lost. If one of you has a clearly stronger forehand or a weaker backhand, stacking so the forehands meet in the middle is worth testing.

The tradeoff. Stacking full time on both serve and return adds movement and a chance to get caught mid switch, which at 3.5 can cost more points than it wins if you are not drilled on it. Never debut it cold on August 23.

The test plan, decide by August 9. Between now and Aug 9, play games with Dylan both ways, some sets stacked and some in your natural sides, and keep it simple by starting with a half stack on the return only. Judge it on three questions. Did the middle get easier to cover or harder. Did you lose points to confusion or bad switches. Did either of you feel rushed getting into position. If stacking does not clearly win more points than it costs by Aug 9, drop it and play natural sides on match day. A simple decision you have drilled beats a clever idea you are still figuring out at the tournament.

The legal basis, updated 07.20.2026

The rule that makes all of this work is smaller than people assume. On the serve, only the server has to be in the correct position. The partner may stand anywhere, including completely off the court. On the return, only the correct returner matters, and again the partner can stand anywhere. Every stack variation below is built on that one allowance. Source: Briones Pickleball Academy, Stacking 101, pulled 07.19.2026.

The four variations, and which one you actually start with

Traditional stack on serve. Dylan lines up on the same side as the server, right next to you. You serve, then slide to your preferred side while he slides the other way. Low movement, easy to learn, and this is the one you start with. It is the whole assignment for Week 2.

Switch stack on serve. The server moves in front and the partner swings behind them. Fixed rule, server in front, partner behind, so nobody collides. Its advantage over the traditional stack is disguise, opponents cannot see in advance where each of you will end up, so they cannot pick one of you to target on the return. Add this only once the traditional version needs no thought.

Return stacking, traditional and switch. Harder, and more ground to cover. The non returning partner stands wide, off the court if needed, to leave the returner a clean line. The returner hits and then runs cross court to the kitchen while the partner slides in. The catch is that this only works if the return is deep and has height, because that is what buys the time to cover the extra distance. A low skidding return does not give you enough time and the stack collapses. This is why the earlier note here recommending a return half stack has been revised: for August 23 treat return stacking as optional, and only run it in a week where your deep return is genuinely reliable.

Hand signals

Behind the back, open hand means switch, closed hand means stay. The point of signals is that you stack only some of the time. A team that stacks every single point is a predictable team, and predictability is worth more to your opponents than the forehand positioning is worth to you. Agree these with Dylan before the first session, not during it.

10. Weights, general and your call

the physics, and what to do with this paddle

The general topic. Adding weight to a paddle changes three things, its total mass, its swingweight, which is how hard it is to swing and rotate about your hand, and its twistweight, how much it resists twisting on off center hits. Where you put the weight decides which one you change. Weight up at the tip adds power, plow through, and stability but raises swingweight the most and slows your hands. Weight at the sides at 3 and 9 adds stability and forgiveness on off center hits with a smaller swingweight cost. Weight low in the handle acts as a counterweight, it barely touches swingweight and makes the head feel lighter and quicker.

Your call. Your Perseus IV is already long, already on the heavier higher swingweight side, and you already find it heavy, and you want fast hands for a tournament full of speed up battles. So do not add weight up top, it would make a heavy paddle heavier to swing and slow the exact hands you need. Leave it stock for now, and if anything add just a couple of grams in the handle or butt to make the head feel lighter and your hands quicker without losing stability. Save any real weight experiment for the off season, not tournament week, same rule as the grip. See the answer on weights and hand speed for the full physics.

11. Tournament prep, your first tournament, and playing with Dylan

the road to August 23

The plan to August 23, week by week

Here is the road to August 23, and it peaks then tapers so you show up fresh. The early weeks are about your soft game, then we add team play, then we sharpen.

Now through July 20 is your foundation, and since you are traveling it is film study and light work, drops and speed up defense in your head and on video. The week of July 21 is doubles patterns with Dylan, sides, stacking, and simple signals. The week of July 28 is team third shots and poaching, both of you to the kitchen together, calling the middle.

The week of August 4 is competitive reps, play games with Dylan and run your patterns, and note what the bracket punishes. The week of August 11 is your peak, match simulation under pressure with the soft game locked in. The final week, August 18 to 23, is taper and compete, cut the volume, keep it crisp, sleep and fuel well, rest Friday and Saturday, and play Sunday.

Playing with Dylan, team patterns that win at 3.5

With Dylan, the points you win at 3.5 come from playing as a team, not from hero shots. Get to the kitchen together and move as a pair, never one up and one back.

Decide your sides early and whether to stack to keep both forehands in the middle. Call the middle on every ball, the forehand in the middle takes it, no hesitation, and talk constantly, mine, yours, switch, bounce it, out. On serve go deep, on return go deep and get in, and let the returner's partner crash the kitchen.

On the third shot, if either of you is behind the baseline, drive and come in, and only drop from inside the baseline. Poach the floaty ball when you read it and cover for each other when one of you goes. Pick on the weaker opponent and the middle, that is where 3.5 teams break down. And when you get sped up, reset together, do not both reach for the counter.

Dylan, his actual profile and the roles that follow from it

What Dylan brings. Incredible reach and length, so he covers a lot of court, he is quick, he jumps well, and he has some power without being a pure banger. What he struggles with is consistency, and he sometimes overcommits. Right now he is slightly better than you overall. None of that is a knock, it is just the shape of the team, and a good team is built on the shape rather than on wishes.

So the roles pick themselves. Dylan takes anything above and behind you both. Overheads are his, lobs over the middle are his, and he is your primary poacher because the reach and the hops turn a floaty ball into a put away. Call it out loud early in every match so there is no hesitation, because two players both leaving an overhead is how 3.5 teams lose easy points.

Your job is to be the anchor. His weakness is consistency, so yours has to be reliability. You own the soft game, the drops, the resets, and the patient dinking that keeps a point alive until his athleticism can end it. Every ball you keep low and in play is a chance for him to do something athletic. If you both try to be the finisher, his inconsistency and your bangy habit compound and you beat yourselves.

Cover behind him when he overcommits. This is the specific team skill to drill. When Dylan poaches or lunges for a ball, he vacates space, so your instinct should be to slide toward the space he left rather than to watch his shot. Practice it, one of you goes and the other rotates. The pair that covers for each other beats the pair with the better individual shots, and this is the single highest value pattern for your particular team.

Your first ever tournament, August 23

This matters more than any shot on this page, and it is easy to underrate. August 23 is your first tournament ever. You have no DUPR yet, so this is also where your rating baseline gets set. That is not pressure, it is the opposite, it means you have nothing to defend and everything to learn, and the number that comes out of it is a starting point rather than a verdict.

What is actually different from open play. You will play several matches in one day against teams that are trying, with waiting between them, on a schedule you do not control. The unfamiliar parts are the pacing and the waiting, not the pickleball. Expect the first match to feel faster and louder than you are used to, and expect your hands to be tighter than normal, which is exactly why the soft game is the thing to lean on rather than power.

Nerves are a plan, not a feeling. Get to the venue early and hit before you play, because a cold first match is the most common way a first timer loses one they should win. 12. Coach answers to your six questions

the ones you asked on the flight, reasoned out

1. Weights and hand speed

You are basically right, and here is the real physics. How fast you can whip the paddle in a firefight is set by its swingweight, the moment of inertia about the pivot near your hand, and every bit of mass adds to that by its distance from the pivot squared. So mass far from your hand, up at the tip, adds a lot of swingweight and slows your hands, while mass near your hand adds almost none. With zero added weight your swingweight is lowest, so yes, no weight at all is the fastest possible hands, all else equal.

The advice to add weight and move it down does not beat adding nothing for pure hand speed, it beats adding weight up high. Weight low in the handle or butt acts as a counterweight, it barely touches swingweight and makes the head feel lighter and quicker, so people use it to offset weight they added elsewhere for stability, or just to make a head heavy paddle feel fast. Weight up at 12 oclock adds power and plow through and stability but costs you hand speed. So your model is correct, none is fastest, low is nearly as fast and more stable, high is more power but slower.

For you specifically, your Perseus IV is already long, already heavier and higher swingweight, and you want fast hands for a tournament full of speed up battles, so do not add weight up top. Leave it stock, or at most add a couple of grams in the handle to make the head feel lighter and your hands quicker. Save any real weight experiments for the off season, not tournament week.

2. The triangle move

It is sound as a slight cheat, not a full commit. The logic holds, most players return your speed up to your backhand because it is the harder wing to defend, and in a kitchen firefight three of the four zones you must cover, the two body balls and the backhand side, are backhand and only one is forehand, so pre setting your paddle a touch toward the backhand ready position genuinely quickens your best reply.

The risk you named is real, a hard ball back to your forehand can catch you leaning, but because only one of four spots is forehand and the forehand side is your stronger natural reach anyway, the odds favor the cheat. So use it, bias slightly toward the backhand, keep your contact out front, and keep enough of a neutral base to rotate the forearm to the one forehand ball. Use it when you are in or expecting a fast exchange, not on ordinary dinks, and never lean so far that a forehand ball is unreachable.

3. One hand vs two hand backhand

Keep both, but build the two hander as your default under pressure. The two handed backhand gives you more stability, disguise, and topspin, it is the Ben Johns roll off both wings, and it holds up better in a fast exchange because the second hand steadies the face. The one hander gives you more reach and a quicker release, which you want on a wide dink, a stretch reset, or a low ball out to the side where a second hand cannot reach.

So the recommendation is not to pick one, it is to have a clear rule, two hands whenever you have time and the ball is in front of you, especially on drives, rolls, and counters, and one hand when you are reaching, stretched, or resetting a wide low ball. Drill the two hander enough that it is your instinct when you are set, and you get the best of both.

4. Speed up timing and pop ups

Your logic is right and worth leaning on. A late speed up, contact behind you, sends the ball up because the face is still opening, so late equals high. That cuts both ways, if you speed up at an opponent who is not set with the paddle up, expect their reply to come back high too, because they are rushed and late just like you would be. At 3.5 most players will not block a surprise speed up low, they pop it up at least chest high or send it back with pace but still up.

So the way to exploit it is to expect the high ball and be ready to end the point. When you speed up at an unprepared opponent, do not admire it, get your paddle right back up and in front and shift your attention to the likely floaty reply, then put it away at their feet or the open body. The speed up is the setup, the put away on their high return is the point. And the flip side, when you are the one caught late, do not add to it, reset it soft.

5. Hands battle, cross court or the player in front

Target the player directly in front of you, not cross court. Two reasons. First, distance and time, a ball straight ahead reaches them faster and gives them less time to react, while a cross court ball travels farther and hands them extra time, the opposite of what you want when you are trying to overwhelm someone. Second, angle, going at the body of the person in front, at their paddle side hip or dominant shoulder, jams them into a cramped weak reply, and it keeps the ball in front of you so you can take the next one out of the air.

Cross court in a firefight also opens the middle behind you and invites their partner to counter. So in a hands battle go straight, at the hip or shoulder of the person across from you, stay compact, and be the first to hit down. Pick on the weaker of the two if you have the choice.

6. Perfecting the hands battle

Winning fast hands comes down to five things, and none of them is swinging harder. One, paddle up and out in front before the exchange starts, between chest and eye level, so the blocking face is already in the ball path and does not have to travel. Two, compact, punch the ball, no backswing, let their pace do the work and just redirect it. Three, know when to counter and when to reset, at or above net height take it and hit down, below the net reset it soft and low and live to the next ball, because countering a low ball feeds their downward angle.

Four, targets, go at the body and the paddle side hip of the person in front, or the middle to cause a whos ball moment, not cross court. Five, base and eyes, stay on your feet, do not lunge forward, keep your eyes near ball level, and cheat your paddle slightly to the backhand since that is where most balls come. Drill it live, dink then either player fires any time, first clean ball down at the feet wins, and it becomes reflex.

14. Training for pickleball in the gym

Planet Fitness only, and nothing that looks silly

The brief was simple, build strength that transfers to the court without looking dumb doing it. So there are no balance boards, no BOSU, no band theatrics and no miming your paddle swing with a cable. Everything here is a normal movement you would see anyone doing on a commercial gym floor, and all of it works with Planet Fitness equipment, machines, a Smith, dumbbells and cables.

What pickleball actually demands physically

Six things. Lateral movement and change of direction, since almost nothing on a court is a straight sprint. Single leg strength and the ability to DECELERATE, because stopping is what blows out knees and ankles and it is what lets you arrive balanced instead of lunging. Rotation and anti rotation through the trunk, because every drive and roll is transmitted through the core. Lower body power within the limits of the equipment you have. Durability at the shoulder, elbow and wrist, which matters a lot with your volume ramp and a heavy elongated paddle. And enough straight hypertrophy work to keep building muscle, since that is still goal number two.

What was added and why

DB lateral lunge, Wednesday. The single most on point lift for pickleball. It loads the exact pattern of pushing out wide for a ball and pushing back to center, and it builds the adductors and lateral hip that a court punishes. Looks like a lunge, because it is one.

DB split squat or step up, Wednesday. Single leg strength plus deceleration. Control the way down slowly, that eccentric IS the deceleration training, and it is what keeps your knees healthy at 5 to 6 sessions a week.

Cable chop, high to low and low to high, Thursday. Trained rotation through the trunk with the hips driving, which is the same chain as a drive or a roll volley. It is a normal cable movement, not a swing impression.

DB farmer carry, Thursday. Grip, forearm, shoulder stability and trunk bracing in one, and grip endurance is quietly relevant when you are holding a paddle for three hours. Just pick up two heavy dumbbells and walk.

Cable external rotation, Friday. Boring, unglamorous, and the single best insurance policy for your shoulder given the volume you are about to add. Ten minutes a week here prevents months off later.

Two free upgrades that cost you nothing

Press the LEG PRESS with intent. Controlled on the way down, then drive fast on the way up. Without a rack you cannot do Olympic lifts or jump squats safely loaded, so intentional speed on the leg press is your power work, and it costs nothing to change.

If Thursday runs long, trim it rather than skipping it. You currently run five separate ab stations, which is more crunching than anyone needs. Keep two, and spend the recovered time on the chop, the carry and the Pallof press, which are the ones that actually transfer.

How to adopt this

Nothing was removed from your split, so nothing you have logged is lost and nothing breaks. These five are additions you can fold in one at a time over the next few weeks while you keep making your own tweaks. Start with the lateral lunge and the external rotation, those two carry the most value per minute. And keep the priority straight, pickleball performance comes first, so as August 23 gets close and on heavy play days, pull the leg volume back rather than pushing through, because arriving fresh beats arriving strong.

18. Hand speed at the kitchen, and what the gym can and cannot do

the honest hierarchy, on court first and gym second

You said hands battles at the kitchen are a struggle, and you also said something correct that most people get wrong: reaction time is largely an eye and nervous system thing. You are right, and I am not going to oversell what lifting can do here. This section is the honest version.

What you are actually solving

Two different things get called hand speed and they have different fixes. The first is REACTION, how long it takes you to recognise a ball is coming at you and start moving. That is perceptual and neural. It improves by seeing real balls at real speed over and over, and it barely responds to gym work at all. The second is what happens after you have reacted, whether your paddle can get to the ball, stay stable through contact, and be back in front of you before the next ball. That part is mechanical, it is mostly position and readiness, and a small slice of it does respond to training in the gym.

Most of what feels like slow hands at 3.5 is neither of those. It is the paddle not being up before the ball is struck. If your paddle starts at your hip, no amount of reaction speed or forearm strength saves you. That is why the ready triangle sits at the top of this list and the gym sits at the bottom.

The hierarchy, in order of how much it will actually help you

One, READY POSITION. Paddle up and in front in the triangle before every ball is struck, not after. This is free, it is available today, and it is worth more than everything else on this list combined. If you fix nothing else, fix this.

Two, ON COURT REPS AT SPEED. Hands battles to a body, dink dink bang, rapid fire feeds from a partner or the club ball machine, and the wall drill. These train the actual perceptual skill against real ball flight, which is the thing that transfers. Two of these are now in your drill list, the wall volley drill and the rapid fire hands drill, and they are the highest transfer items you have access to.

Three, GRIP PRESSURE AND ABSORPTION. A loose grip that firms only at contact is faster than a tight one and it is what lets you block rather than swing. This is a skill and a habit, not a strength quality.

Four, THE GYM. Last, and genuinely last. Worth two short blocks a week, worth roughly what a small percentage improvement is worth, and not worth taking time away from anything above it.

What the gym can plausibly do, in rough order of transfer

Forearm, wrist and grip work done LIGHT and FAST rather than heavy and slow. Paddle control under pace is a real limiter for you, and the quality that matters is how well you can hold a stable face against a ball arriving hard, not how much weight your forearm can move. So wrist flexion and extension, and pronation and supination with a very light dumbbell, all done for speed. Heavy slow wrist curls build a forearm, they do not build a fast one. Your Saturday already had wrist curls, so the fast work now sits alongside them.

Rate of force development for the upper body. Light, explosive cable presses and cable punches at low load with maximum intent, judged entirely on how fast the rep STARTS rather than how much weight moves. The transfer claim here is modest and honest: it may help the very first instant of a defensive movement. It will not make you see the ball sooner.

Scapular and rotator cuff stability, so the shoulder can decelerate and reset quickly between blocks. In a hands battle you are not making one movement, you are making four or five in two seconds, and the limiter is often how fast you can stop and re cock rather than how fast you can go. External rotations and cable work. You already have face pulls and reverse pec deck, so this is a small addition rather than a new pillar.

Anti rotation and trunk stiffness. A stable trunk is what lets your hands move independently of your body, and a soft trunk means every hand movement drags your torso with it. Pallof press and cable rotations are already in your Thursday, so this one is already covered and just needs to be understood as hand speed work rather than ab work.

Equipment reality

All of this is built for Planet Fitness. Cables, machines, the Smith machine and light dumbbells only. No barbells, and no medicine balls, since PF usually does not stock them, so anything you read elsewhere about med ball chest passes for rate of force development gets replaced by the light explosive cable press.

The thing I do not want you to take away

Do not read this section and conclude that you can lift your way to faster hands, because you cannot, and a version of you that spends an extra twenty minutes on wrist work instead of twenty minutes of rapid fire feeds has gone backwards. The gym blocks are there because they are cheap, they take five minutes, they fit into days you are already training, and they plausibly help a little at the margins. The court is where this actually gets fixed.
